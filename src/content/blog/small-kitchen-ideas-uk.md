British kitchens are notoriously small. Galley kitchens in terraced houses, the tiny kitchen in a converted flat, the awkward L-shape with a boiler cupboard taking up a third of the usable wall space — we've all been there.

The good news is that small kitchens are not the problem they appear. Thoughtful storage and a few styling decisions can transform even the most challenging kitchen into one that genuinely works.

---

## 1. Use Every Inch of Wall Height

The space between the top of kitchen units and the ceiling is almost universally wasted. Options:
- Add an extra run of open shelving (IKEA BOAXEL is very adaptable)
- Use the top of upper cupboards for rarely used items — cake tins, seasonal serving dishes, the bread maker you use twice a year
- If you're fitting new units, go floor to ceiling rather than leaving a gap

---

## 2. Open Shelving — Done Right

Open shelving gets a bad reputation because it's often done badly (cluttered, dusty, mismatched). Done well — with consistent storage containers, properly edited contents — it's one of the most functional and attractive things you can do in a small kitchen.

Keep open shelving to one wall or section. Keep the contents curated: matching pasta jars, one set of frequently used plates, the things you actually reach for daily.

---

## 3. A Magnetic Knife Strip

The knife block takes up precious counter space. A magnetic knife strip on the wall is safer (no dark slots for bacteria), more accessible, and frees up immediate worktop area. IKEA's KUNGSFORS is around £13.

---

## 4. Pegboards for Utensils

A pegboard screwed to the inside of a cupboard door or on a wall section turns hanging space into functional storage. Hang measuring spoons, whisks, scissors, graters — anything that currently lives in a drawer.

---

## 5. The Right Colour Makes It Feel Bigger

In a small kitchen, colour choices have an outsized effect on how spacious the room feels:
- Light colours (white, off-white, pale grey, pale sage) make the room feel larger
- Dark colours (navy, forest green, charcoal) make it feel more intentional and designed, but require good lighting
- Avoid many different colours or patterns — keep it cohesive

---

## 6. Under-Sink Storage

The area under the sink is often chaos — cleaning products, spare bin bags, and mystery items from three tenancies ago. A proper under-sink organiser (search Amazon for "under sink organiser UK," around £20–£30) transforms this space completely.

---

## 7. A Slim Rolling Island

If your kitchen has any floor space that isn't regularly used for movement, a slim rolling island with a butcher block or marble-look top adds both worktop space and storage. When not in use, it rolls against a wall.

IKEA RÅSKOG is often adapted for this purpose, or look at Dunelm's kitchen trolley range.

---

## 8. Good Lighting Changes Everything

Poorly lit kitchens feel smaller and drearier than they need to. Options:
- Under-cabinet LED strip lighting (transforms the feel of the worktop area)
- A proper pendant light over a table or island, separate from the main overhead light
- Replace any fluorescent strip lights with LED panels with a warm temperature (3000K)

---

## 9. Decant and Edit

The biggest storage gains in a small kitchen come from what you remove, not what you add. A full kitchen edit — going through every cupboard and removing duplicates, unused gadgets, and long-expired ingredients — typically frees 20–30% more space.

Then decant dry goods into matching containers. Glass Kilner jars or matching IKEA RAJTAN containers make a shelf of different-sized packets into something that looks deliberate and attractive.

---

A small kitchen that's properly organised and thoughtfully styled feels significantly larger than its square footage suggests. The effort is almost entirely about systems and decisions rather than money.
