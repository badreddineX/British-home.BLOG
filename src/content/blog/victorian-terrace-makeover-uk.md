Victorian terraced houses are among the most common property types in the UK. Built between roughly 1837 and 1901, they're found across every major British city — and they come with a set of features that, when treated correctly, give them a character that new builds simply cannot replicate.

The problem is that many Victorian terraces have had their features stripped out, painted over, or obscured by renovation choices that worked against the architecture rather than with it. This is a guide to making a Victorian terrace feel like what it is: a genuinely beautiful piece of British architectural heritage.

---

## Work With the Ceiling Height

Victorian terraces typically have higher ceilings than modern properties — often 2.6–2.8m in ground-floor rooms. This is an enormous advantage that many owners fail to exploit.

**How to use it:**
- Hang curtains from as close to the ceiling as possible, not from just above the window
- Choose tall furniture — bookcases and wardrobes that go to ceiling height
- Use pendant lights that hang at least 60–80cm below the ceiling
- Don't be afraid of larger artwork — Victorian rooms can carry it

---

## The Cornicing

Original Victorian plaster cornicing is one of the most beautiful things a room can have. If yours survives, paint it in an off-white (Farrow & Ball *Pointing* or Dulux *Natural Hessian*) and make it the frame for everything else.

If the cornicing has been removed, replica plaster coving from companies like Locker & Riley or Georgian Period Ornaments can be cut to size and fitted for a few hundred pounds — it's one of the best value improvements you can make.

---

## The Fireplace as Focal Point

If you have an original Victorian fireplace, it should be the clear focal point of the main reception room. If it's been blocked up:
- A freestanding cast iron Victorian-style insert (available from Cast Iron Radiators Co., from around £150)
- Or a simple chimney breast treatment: tile or slate panel, a wooden mantelpiece

If it's original but dated: strip it back to the cast iron, have it cleaned and blacked, and replace any tiles that are cracked with period-appropriate replacements (Fired Earth and Mandarin Stone have good period reproduction ranges).

---

## Floor Treatments

Victorian terraces were built with beautiful floors:
- Encaustic geometric tiles in hallways (still found in many properties — restore them by cleaning with a mild acid and sealing)
- Floorboards throughout (often pine, sometimes wider boards in older properties)

If your boards are exposed: sand and seal them. A lightly sanded, lightly oiled pine floor in a Victorian terrace is one of the most beautiful things in British interior design. It costs a weekend and around £100 in materials.

If they're carpeted: lifting and sanding almost always pays off unless the boards underneath are in very poor condition.

---

## Colour Choices for Period Rooms

Victorian rooms suit colours with depth and historical precedent. This doesn't mean everything needs to be dark:

- **Drawing rooms:** *Porphyry Pink*, *Arsenic*, *Hague Blue*, *Pitch Black* (all Farrow & Ball) suit the formality of a front reception room
- **Back rooms:** warmer, softer — *String*, *Elephant's Breath*, *Mizzle*, *Old White*
- **Hallways:** strong colour works beautifully in hallways that are walked through rather than lived in — *Railings*, *Down Pipe*, or a good terracotta

---

## What to Avoid

- **Downlights throughout:** spotlights in the ceiling of a Victorian room look deeply incongruous. Stick to pendant lights and lamps
- **Very modern furniture that fights the architecture:** mid-century modern generally works; ultra-contemporary glass-and-steel does not
- **Carpeting over original floors:** almost always a mistake
- **Removing period details for a "cleaner" look:** the details are the asset

---

## The Approach

The most successful Victorian terrace interiors I've seen don't try to modernise the house — they work with what's there, restore what's been lost, and furnish in a way that feels coherent with the bones of the building.

Think of it less as decorating and more as collaboration with a building that already has strong opinions about itself.
