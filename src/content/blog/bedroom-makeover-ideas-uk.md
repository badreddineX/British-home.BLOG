This is the bedroom makeover I wish someone had written when I was staring at a magnolia box room wondering where to start.

The room in question: a second bedroom in a Victorian terrace. 10ft × 10ft, single window facing east, fitted wardrobe on one wall that couldn't be removed (rented), magnolia walls, no overhead lighting (just a pendant bulb hanging from the ceiling rose), beige carpet that had seen better decades.

Total budget: £350. Time: one long weekend plus a few evenings.

---

## The Problems

Before spending anything, I listed exactly what was wrong:

1. The colour (magnolia) made the room feel tired before anything else
2. The lighting was appalling — one bare bulb
3. The bed had no headboard and looked unanchored
4. The wardrobe was dark wood laminate and dated the whole room
5. No softness — the room felt functional but not inviting

This list is important because it tells you where to spend the money.

---

## The Plan

| Fix | Solution | Approximate Cost |

## Day 1: Paint

I chose Dulux *Timeless Tranquility* — a warm sage green that reads quite differently depending on the light. In the morning, with the east-facing sun, it glows with a gentle gold. In the evening under warm artificial light, it's deep and calm.

The wardrobe got Crown *Chalk White* — one coat of Zinsser BIN primer (critical on laminate) and two coats of the topcoat. Painting the wardrobe the same pale colour as the woodwork made it recede visually. It went from dominating the room to almost disappearing into it.

Painting tip: remove drawer fronts and doors, paint them flat on the floor. The result is ten times better than painting in place.

---

## Day 2: Lighting

The bare bulb went. In its place: a rattan pendant from Dunelm (£28) with a warm 4W LED golf ball bulb (2700K — critical for warmth).

Two bedside lamps from IKEA's SKOTTORP range at £12 each with warm-white bulbs. Suddenly the room had three light sources, all warm, all controllable.

The effect of proper layered bedroom lighting on the feel of a room cannot be overstated. This was the single most impactful change I made.

---

## Day 2 Afternoon: The Headboard

A tall upholstered headboard from Argos (£85) attached directly to the wall — took 45 minutes to install. The instant the headboard went up, the bed looked like a piece of furniture rather than a mattress sitting on a frame.

I chose a muted sage-grey velvet that sat in the same tonal family as the walls — just slightly darker and with more blue in it.

---

## Day 3: Bedding and the Finishing Details

New duvet set from John Lewis (£55 for a full set — their own brand is consistently good). I chose oatmeal linen-look cotton — warm, neutral, pairs with anything. Added two extra pillowcases in the same oatmeal and a sage-green cushion cover.

The throw — a warm caramel chunky knit from H&M Home (around £25 at the time) — went across the foot of the bed.

Rug: a grey-brown wool-look rectangle from IKEA (£45) placed half under the bed on the window side. This is where you step out of bed in the morning — it deserves to be comfortable and warm.

Plants: one pothos on the windowsill in a terracotta pot (£5 for the pot, £8 for the plant) and a trailing heartleaf philodendron on the top of the wardrobe.

---

## The Result

The room that felt like a spare room now feels like a hotel room you actually want to stay in. The walls are a colour rather than a non-colour. The lighting has warmth and atmosphere. The bed looks like it belongs.

Total cost: £355.
Total time: one weekend, spread across three days.

The things that helped most, in order:
1. The lighting (instant and dramatic)
2. The paint (changed the mood of the whole room)
3. The headboard (made the bed into a proper piece of furniture)

The things that helped least: the accessories. Lovely but not transformative. If you're budget-limited, spend on the first three and skip the last.

---

## What I'd Do Differently

I should have done the wardrobe first, before the walls — the primer fumes were unpleasant and I had to repaint a small section of wall I'd already finished. Other than that: nothing. Every pound felt well spent.
