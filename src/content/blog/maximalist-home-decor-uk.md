There's a version of maximalism that looks chaotic, dated, and exhausting. And then there's the version that makes you walk into a room and feel immediately, deeply at home — surrounded by colour, texture, objects with stories, and the specific pleasure of a space that doesn't apologise for itself.

British homes are, arguably, made for maximalism. Victorian terraces with original features, bay windows, fireplaces, and picture rails — these architectural bones practically beg to be dressed properly. Even a modern flat can carry a maximalist interior if you understand the underlying rules.

I've been layering my own living room for three years, adding and editing, and I've learned that the difference between beautiful maximalism and overwhelming clutter is smaller than you'd think.

**TL;DR:** Anchor your scheme with two or three dominant colours, mix patterns within that palette, group objects intentionally, and layer textures before you layer anything else. Done right, maximalism creates the most characterful rooms in Britain — and most of it can be sourced from charity shops, Etsy, and Dunelm for under £200.

---

## 1. Start with Colour, Not Objects

The single most common maximalist mistake is buying things first and trying to create a coherent scheme around them later. It never works.

Before you buy anything, choose your colour story. Maximalism doesn't mean every colour — it means all your chosen colours, used boldly and repeatedly. A maximalist room anchored in forest green, burnt orange, and cream reads as sophisticated. The same room with those colours plus coral, lavender, and cobalt reads as chaos.

**How to pick your palette:** Pull three to four colours from something you already love — a vintage rug, a piece of art, a favourite piece of clothing. Those are your anchor colours. Everything else in the room should contain at least one of them.

I painted one wall in my living room in Farrow & Ball *Hague Blue* and built the entire scheme outward from that. Suddenly every object — the amber ceramic lamp, the terracotta cushions, the brass picture frames — found its place without any effort.

## 2. Understand Pattern Mixing

Pattern mixing is the skill that separates a genuinely good maximalist interior from a headache-inducing one. The good news is that it's a learnable skill with clear rules.

**Rule one: vary the scale.** A large-scale floral and a small-scale geometric can live together. Two large-scale patterns fight each other.

**Rule two: keep the colour family consistent.** A striped cushion in forest green, burnt orange, and cream will work with a floral cushion in those same tones, even if the patterns are completely different.

**Rule three: use solids as breathers.** Every three or four patterned items, introduce one solid in your dominant colour. This gives the eye a place to rest without breaking the layered effect.

Dunelm's cushion range is brilliant for experimenting with this — you can pick up patterned cushions from around £8 each and swap them out until you find the right combination without spending a fortune.

## 3. Layer Textures Before You Layer Objects

This is the foundation of a maximalist room that feels intentional rather than cluttered: texture comes first, objects second.

Think of it as building from the floor up. Start with a large rug in a rich tone or pattern — a Persian-style rug from Wayfair UK (from £65) or a vintage find from a car boot sale. Add curtains in velvet or heavy linen. Then layer cushions in a mix of velvet, cotton, and embroidered fabric. Then add throws.

Only once you've built the textile layer do you start adding objects. By then, the room already feels full, warm, and layered — and you add objects with restraint rather than desperation.

**The textures to aim for:** rough (jute, wicker), smooth (velvet, lacquered ceramics), soft (wool, linen), and hard (brass, wood, marble). Having all four categories present makes a room feel genuinely rich.

---

## 4. Group Objects Intentionally

The difference between a beautiful vignette and a cluttered shelf comes down to grouping. Maximalism works when objects are arranged in deliberate compositions, not scattered randomly across surfaces.

**The rule of odd numbers:** Group objects in threes or fives rather than twos and fours. Odd-numbered groupings read as more natural and visually interesting.

**The rule of varying height:** Every group needs a tall element, a medium element, and a low element. A tall vase, a medium candlestick, a small ceramic figure — done. This creates visual movement that draws the eye without creating confusion.

**The rule of one empty space per surface:** Even in maximalism, each surface should have at least one breathing gap. This is what separates a maximalist display from actual clutter.

I've found the best objects for maximalist groupings at charity shops — Oxfam and the British Heart Foundation regularly have interesting ceramics, brass candlesticks, old books, and glass vases. A full display can cost under £20 if you're patient.

## 5. Use Gallery Walls Deliberately

A gallery wall is the maximalist's most powerful tool — and also the one most likely to go wrong. The key is committing to a frame style and a colour palette, then letting the content vary.

Two approaches that work consistently:

**Matching frames, varied content:** All frames in the same finish — all black, all gold, all natural wood — with a mix of art, prints, photographs, and botanical illustrations inside. The consistency of the frames holds the wall together.

**Mixed frames, consistent tone:** Frames in different sizes and styles but all within one colour family (all dark wood tones, or all metallic tones). The variety becomes the point.

For renters: Command Picture Hanging Strips from B&Q handle frames up to 3.6kg per pair without damaging walls. You can build an entire gallery wall without a single nail. See our [rented flat makeover guide](/blog/rented-flat-makeover-uk/) for more deposit-safe ideas.

## 6. Don't Forget the Ceiling

Most people stop at the picture rail and ignore everything above. In a maximalist room, the ceiling is an opportunity.

A statement ceiling — painted in a deep tone or wallpapered with a bold pattern — adds another layer of richness without using any floor or wall space. In a Victorian terrace, a dark sage or navy ceiling with ornate coving creates exactly the kind of layered grandeur that maximalism is built on.

If you're renting, a removable ceiling rose sticker and fairy lights strung above a doorway achieve a similar effect with zero permanence.

---

## 7. How Many Plants Is Too Many?

In a maximalist room: there is no too many. Plants are the element that brings a maximalist interior to life — they add colour, texture, scale, and actual living presence to a space.

The key is varying the type. A tall fiddle-leaf fig in the corner, a trailing pothos on a high shelf, a cluster of smaller succulents on a windowsill, and a fern on a plant stand by the fireplace — this creates a layered botanical landscape rather than a collection of pots.

According to a 2023 survey by the RHS, 74% of UK adults agree that plants make a home feel more welcoming. I'd go further: in a maximalist interior, they're load-bearing.

Local garden centres and IKEA (where most common houseplants start at around £3–£5) are the best sources. Resist buying huge plants at garden centre prices when small ones grow quickly with basic care.

---

## 8. The Maximalist Bookshelf

Books are one of the best decorative objects in a maximalist room — they add colour, texture, and the suggestion of a lived life. But a maximalist bookshelf isn't just books stacked vertically.

Try this arrangement: two-thirds books arranged by colour spine-out, one-third books stacked horizontally with a small object on top (a candle, a small plant, a ceramic figure), and occasional gaps for vases or interesting bottles. The horizontal stacks create visual rhythm and give you places to tuck in small objects.

IKEA's KALLAX units are the maximalist's best friend — modular, affordable (from ~£55), and endlessly customisable with mix of open and closed units.

---

## 9. Maximalism on a Budget

Full maximalism — the layered, rich, abundant version — doesn't require a large budget. It requires patience and the willingness to build slowly.

**Where to shop:**
- **Charity shops:** Oxfam, BHF, Sue Ryder — ceramics, frames, books, candles, small furniture
- **Etsy UK:** Vintage botanical prints, handmade ceramics, unusual textiles
- **Dunelm:** Cushions, throws, curtains — good price-to-quality ratio
- **IKEA UK:** Shelving, basic furniture, plant pots
- **Wayfair UK:** Rugs, statement lamps, occasional furniture
- **Facebook Marketplace:** Large furniture at fraction of retail

The maximalist room I'm most proud of cost me less than £300 to build — and about two years of slow, intentional layering. That's the honest version of maximalism that works in British homes.
