Kitchen trends move more slowly than fashion trends — which is exactly as it should be, given the cost of a kitchen renovation. But they do move. And 2026 has brought some distinct shifts in what British homeowners are choosing.

Here's an honest breakdown of what's working, what's not, and what will still look good in five years' time.

---

## In: Forest Green and Deep Sage

Green kitchens have been growing in popularity for several years and in 2026 they've become the dominant choice in kitchen renovations across the UK. Not any green — specifically the deeper, more serious greens:

- **Forest green** (Farrow & Ball *Calke Green*, Dulux *Forest Green*, or Howdens *Hunter Green*)
- **Deep sage** (softer, slightly grey-green — Fired Earth *Sage Leaf*, B&Q *Urban Jungle*)

What makes these work: they're warm enough to avoid feeling cold in British light, they pair beautifully with brass and gold hardware, and they look genuinely expensive.

---

## In: Warm Clay and Terracotta

A reaction to the cool greys and whites of the previous decade, warm clay and terracotta kitchen cabinets are having a significant moment. They work particularly well in older homes with natural stone floors or exposed brick.

- IKEA's SEKTION or METOD cabinets painted in Crown's *Warm Clay*
- Howdens *Terracotta* for a warmer, more orange-red option
- Frenchic's *Sandy Toes* for a budget cabinet refresh

The key is pairing with warm-toned countertops — butcher block, warm beige stone, or terracotta tiles.

---

## In: Warm White (Replacing Cool White)

The pure, cool, brilliant white kitchen is fading. In its place: warm whites and off-whites with cream, linen, or even blush undertones. They feel warmer and more lived-in — appropriate for a room that genuinely gets lived in.

- Farrow & Ball *Pointing* remains one of their best whites for kitchen use
- Dulux *White Cotton* — a solid affordable choice
- Shaker-style cabinetry in warm white with integrated handles looks effortlessly timeless

---

## In: Two-Tone Kitchens

Upper cabinets in one colour, lower cabinets in another — this approach has moved from high-end kitchen studios into mainstream renovation choices. The most popular combinations:

- Pale sage uppers + forest green lowers
- White uppers + navy lowers
- Warm cream uppers + clay lowers

The general rule: lighter tone on top, darker on bottom. It feels grounded and proportional.

---

## Out: Greige (Grey-Beige)

The greige kitchen dominated the mid-2010s and is now firmly in the "looks like a 2016 house" category. If you have greige kitchen cabinets, a coat of paint in a warmer, more decisive colour is a relatively simple upgrade.

---

## Out: High-Gloss White

High-gloss white was the peak kitchen trend around 2012–2018. The surfaces show every mark, the look dates badly, and it reads as estate agency neutral rather than a considered choice.

Matt finishes in any colour are far more forgiving and look better over time.

---

## Timeless: Classic Shaker in a Thoughtful Colour

Shaker-style kitchen cabinets — simple recessed panel doors with clean lines — are as close to timeless as kitchen design gets. They work in traditional, contemporary, and country kitchen settings.

The colour you choose on a shaker cabinet can be updated (professionally or as a DIY project) for a fraction of the cost of new cabinets. This flexibility is what makes shaker the most sensible choice for most British homes.

---

## A Note on Following Trends

Kitchen renovations cost significant money. Choosing a colour because it's trending in 2026 and then living with it until 2036 is a risk. The better question to ask: does this colour make me feel good in the morning when I come to make a cup of tea? If yes, it's the right choice regardless of trend.

Classic, considered choices always look better ten years later than clever trend-chasing ones.
